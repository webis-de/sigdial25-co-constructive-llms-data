A full sleep cycle typically lasts around 90-120 minutes and is divided into three stages of non-REM (rapid eye movement) sleep and one stage of REM sleep.
Here's a breakdown of the stages:
**Stage 1: N1 (Non-REM) - Transition to Sleep**
This is the lightest stage of sleep, where you drift in and out of consciousness. Your brain waves slow down, and you may experience a sense of relaxation.
**Stage 2: N2 (Non-REM) - Light Sleep**
In this stage, your heart rate and body temperature decrease, and your brain waves slow down further. Your body starts to prepare for deeper sleep.
**Stage 3: N3 (Non-REM) - Deep Sleep**
This is the deepest stage of non-REM sleep, also known as slow-wave sleep. Your brain waves slow down even more, and it's difficult to wake up during this stage. This is where your body repairs and regenerates tissues, builds bone and muscle, and strengthens your immune system.
**Stage 4: REM (Rapid Eye Movement) Sleep**
This is the stage where you dream. Your brain activity increases, and your eyes move rapidly. Your body is paralyzed to prevent acting out your dreams. REM sleep is important for memory consolidation, learning, and emotional regulation.
A full sleep cycle consists of all four stages, and most people experience 3-5 cycles per night. As you sleep, you'll cycle through these stages multiple times, with each cycle becoming progressively lighter.
It's worth noting that sleep cycles vary across the lifespan. Infants and young children have shorter sleep cycles, while older adults tend to have longer sleep cycles.

When we're awake, our brain builds up a sleep-promoting substance called adenosine. Adenosine is a byproduct of cellular metabolism, and it accumulates in the brain throughout the day. As adenosine levels increase, we start to feel tired and sleepy.
As we fall asleep, adenosine levels decrease, and our brain starts to produce sleep-promoting hormones like melatonin and GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid). These hormones help us relax and fall into a deeper sleep.
**The REM-NREM Cycle:**
During sleep, our brain cycles between REM (rapid eye movement) and NREM (non-rapid eye movement) sleep. REM sleep is characterized by rapid eye movements, increased brain activity, and vivid dreams. NREM sleep is characterized by slower brain waves, reduced body temperature, and slower heart rate.
The REM-NREM cycle is regulated by the brain's internal clock, which is controlled by a group of cells in the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN). The SCN responds to light and darkness to synchronize our sleep-wake cycle with the 24-hour day-night cycle.
**Why Sleep Cycles Repeat:**
Sleep cycles repeat during the night because our brain needs to alternate between REM and NREM sleep to complete the different stages of sleep. Each stage of sleep serves a specific purpose, and our brain needs to cycle through all stages to ensure that we get the restorative sleep we need.
The REM-NREM cycle typically lasts around 90-120 minutes, and we experience 3-5 cycles per night. Each cycle is composed of three stages of NREM sleep and one stage of REM sleep.
**Why We Need Multiple Sleep Cycles:**
We need multiple sleep cycles to ensure that we get the restorative sleep we need. Each sleep cycle is designed to serve a specific purpose:
1. **Stage 1 NREM:** Transition to sleep, relaxation, and reduced body temperature.
